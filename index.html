The Fibonacci sequence, discovered by Leonardo Fibonacci in the 13th century, starts with 0 and 1, where each subsequent number is the sum of the two preceding ones. This simple yet captivating sequence has remarkable connections to nature, architecture, and art, with spirals and ratios appearing in sunflower seeds, seashells, and architectural designs.

The Lucas sequence, named after mathematician François Édouard Anatole Lucas, starts with 2 and 1. Like the Fibonacci sequence, each subsequent number is the sum of the two preceding ones. Though similar, Lucas has distinct initial values, offering unique mathematical properties and applications in fields such as number theory and computer science.

The Tribonacci sequence starts with 0, 1, 1, and each subsequent number is the sum of the three preceding ones. This captivating numerical series reveals an interconnected and cumulative pattern, creating a vibrant mathematical symphony.
